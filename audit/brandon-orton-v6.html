2. What is working right now

Before any of the leaks, name the wins. Most creatives on LinkedIn would trade for what you already have on the page.

  • Featured section is filled. Most creatives leave this empty. You have 4+ items pinned, which is the single highest-value real estate on a LinkedIn profile after the headline.
  • The pinned video has real signal. 41 reactions and 13 comments on the top Featured item. That is social proof a stranger can see in 3 seconds.
  • Recognizable client names are visible. doTERRA on the brand-film side. Children's Service Society of Utah on the nonprofit side. Both names carry weight in their categories. A buyer scanning your page learns you have shipped at scale and you have shipped for cause-driven work.
  • "Creative Director · Filmmaker" is two clean hats. Not ambiguous. You direct, and you make the film. No agency-jargon stack to decode.

The bones are good. What follows is not a rebuild. It is a re-aim.

3. The single biggest leak: stale identity

Your profile sells doTERRA. You no longer work at doTERRA.

The Experience section still lists doTERRA as your current employer. That means LinkedIn auto-fills "Creative Director at doTERRA" into the byline that appears under your name in search results, comment threads, DM previews, and "people you may know" cards. Roughly 90% of the surfaces where someone first sees you are pulled from current-employer auto-text, not from your headline. So even when your headline is right, the byline a stranger sees somewhere else on LinkedIn is wrong.

Until Experience is fixed, every other edit you make gets fought by auto-text.

Featured content does the actual work of proving you ship strong stuff. The problem is the headline and the byline are not promising what the Featured section delivers. A prospect bounces before they get to the proof.

Fix the identity layer first. Everything else compounds after that.

4. The foundation question (answer this first)

Three identity paths. You answer this. Everything downstream branches off the answer.

Recommended · start here

Path A: Solo independent director

Brand is "Brandon Orton." No agency name. You are the product. Lean, honest, ships fast. Experience reads "Independent Director / Filmmaker (Self-Employed)" with the start date you actually went out on your own.

Why this first: zero entity setup, zero name-debates, zero website rework when you change the agency name in 6 months. The 30-day arc finishes inside this path. You can upgrade to Path B later without breaking anything you ship this month.

Upgrade · 6 to 12 months out

Path B: Named agency

Pick a name, register the entity, register the domain, build the agency LinkedIn page. Higher leverage long-term. You can hire under it. Clients can refer their procurement team to a company name instead of a person. Pricing conversations get easier because you are pricing a studio, not your hours.

Why later, not now: the name is the hardest part. You will spend 2 weeks debating it. That is 2 weeks not shipping the case studies that earn the next 3 clients. Earn the volume first, name the agency once the volume justifies the friction.

Dismiss

Path C: Stay employee-flavored, chase staff contracts

Pitch yourself as a Creative Director available for studio or big-brand staff roles. Dismissed because you have already left that lane. The fact that you are running your own client load right now means the in-house path is the wrong fit for the next 12 months. Worth naming so we do not pretend it is on the table.

The rest of this document assumes Path A as the working answer. Where Path B changes the recommendation, I show both side by side. If you pick differently, the same structure still applies, you just swap the labels.

5e · Recommendations

You almost certainly have 1 or 2 recommendations already from the doTERRA tenure. The play is to add 3 more that anchor the current chapter.

  • Ask 1: doTERRA producer or director-tier contact who can name the campaign you directed and the outcome it drove.
  • Ask 2: Children's Service Society of Utah contact who can speak to how the film moved the mission, not just the production quality.
  • Ask 3: the most recent agency client you shipped for. Even if it is small. Recency is the signal.

What to ask them for: "2 to 3 sentences about the outcome the work drove, not the personality I bring to the room." The personality-recs are nice. The outcome-recs are the ones that convert a profile-viewer into a DM.

Template you can paste into a DM:

5f · Activity (posting cadence)

You have under-posted. That is fine. Most directors do. The fix is small.

  • 1 substantive post per week. Case-study, BTS shot, or thought piece on the craft. 4 to 6 paragraphs. One image or short clip.
  • 2 to 3 comment threads per week on others' posts. Other directors, agency owners, brand-side marketers in your network. Substantive comment, not "great post." 2 to 4 sentences.
  • 4 weeks to build a cadence visible enough that profile-viewers see "this person is active here." Not viral. Visible.

The point is not engagement-farming. The point is that when a prospect lands on your profile and clicks Activity, they see proof you are present in the space, not a ghost.

7. Why this is not just a LinkedIn fix

LinkedIn is the discovery layer. Conversion happens off-platform. A prospect who is convinced by your headline, About, and Featured does not buy from LinkedIn. They DM you, you reply, you point them at a website, they look at the website, they email you, you book a call.

No website means a chunk of those convinced prospects fall out of the funnel between "DM you" and "email you." There is nowhere to send them that consolidates the proof. So they go cold.

Fixing LinkedIn without fixing the website is like cleaning the storefront window while the front door is locked.

The 30-day arc below assumes both happen in parallel. That is why the free landing-site offer is in this document and not a separate conversation.
