Before you buy another AI tool, read this.

The most expensive mistake in AI adoption is not the wrong tool. It is skipping the diagnosis. Eighty percent of AI projects fail. Not because of bad AI. Because of broken processes that were already there.

Most operators evaluating AI start at the tool. Wrong end of the rope.

The tool is the last decision in a chain of four. When the chain runs in reverse, tool first and problem last, the AI gets implemented against an undiagnosed constraint. The constraint absorbs the tool. Nothing measurable changes.

The vendor blames adoption. The operator blames the AI. Both are wrong.

The four-step chain in the correct order

  1. Name the operational constraint. What is the one bottleneck slowing the business right now. Not "we need more leads." That is a feeling. "Senior capacity is being burned on work two levels below its pay grade." That is a constraint.

  2. Map the workflow that touches the constraint. Who hands what to whom. Where the friction lives. What gets repeated. What gets dropped. Write it down before any tool conversation starts.

  3. Identify the leverage point. Of the steps in that workflow, which one would deliver the most relief if it ran ten times faster, or did not need a human at all, or could be predicted instead of reacted to.

  4. Pick the tool. Now the tool conversation is short. The constraint and the leverage point have done 90 percent of the selection for you.

"The tool decision is the last decision. Most operators make it first."

Why 80% of AI projects fail

The numbers from McKinsey and Gartner over 2024 and 2025 are consistent. Somewhere between 70 and 85 percent of enterprise AI initiatives never deliver measurable ROI. The headline reads "the AI did not work." The mechanism is upstream.

The pattern repeats. A procurement team picks a tool because it is what the competitor uses. The constraint inside their own business is never named. The tool gets deployed against the wrong workflow. The pilot reports low adoption. The vendor blames change management. The operator blames the model.

The actual cause is different. The AI did exactly what it was asked to do. Against a problem that was not the constraint.

What this looks like in practice

A CFO buys an AI accounts-payable tool because invoices are slow. The AI processes invoices flawlessly. Six months later, invoice cycle-time is unchanged. The constraint was never AP. It was the approval routing one step upstream. A workflow problem disguised as a data problem.

A founder buys an AI sales tool to improve conversion. Conversion does not move. The constraint was never the rep's pitch. It was the lead qualification one stage earlier. The wrong leads were being passed to the rep. Better software does not fix a wrong input.

Before you buy another AI tool

Spend the first 90 minutes naming the constraint. Then the tool decision takes 20 minutes. Most operators do the opposite. Most operators are wrong.

The diagnosis is the work. The tool is the receipt. Run the chain in the correct order and the failure rate inverts. Skip the diagnosis and you join the 80 percent.

That is the whole letter. Read it twice before the next vendor demo.
