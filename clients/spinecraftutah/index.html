03 What is actually blocking AI from citing you

Five mechanical issues. None are about the writing. The writing is fine. The issues are about the format the AI engines need to ingest the writing.

1. Zero schema.org structured data

There is no JSON-LD on the homepage, the contact page, or the auto-accidents page. Schema is the format AI engines and Google Knowledge Graph read to extract: clinic name, address, hours, phone, services, doctor's name, accepted insurance, areas served. Without it, the engines have to guess by scraping prose. They mostly skip.

What you need: MedicalBusiness on the homepage and contact page, Physician on Dr. Wheeler's bio (he does not have a bio page yet, see issue 3), Service on each treatment page, FAQPage on a Provo-FAQ block, Article on every blog post. About 60 lines of JSON-LD per page type. One-time fix.

2. Blog and testimonials content is JavaScript-rendered

When I fetched /blog and /testimonials directly, the pages returned the section header but no actual post content or testimonial text. The content loads via JavaScript after the page renders. AI crawlers and many search bots do not execute JavaScript reliably. They see the empty shell. Your 22 blog posts are functionally invisible to half the bots that look at them.

What you need: server-side render the blog post content into the initial HTML. Same for testimonials. This is typically a one-setting flip on Wix/EditorX or a small theme adjustment.

3. No team page, no doctor profile page

Dr. Wheeler's credentials appear in homepage prose. There is no /team or /dr-wheeler page. Google's Knowledge Graph and Claude both prefer to cite a doctor's name with their clinic when they have a dedicated profile to crawl. Today they have prose buried in a hero section.

What you need: a /team/dr-rob-wheeler page with the Physician schema attached, a headshot (not stock), the 30-year story, the Expert Medical Witness credential, the Alaska and Florida history, and the conditions he specialises in.

4. No FAQ block on any high-intent page

FAQ blocks with FAQPage schema are the single highest-ROI move for AI citation. Each Q&A becomes an answer the AI can pull verbatim. Right now none of your service pages (auto accidents, chiropractic care, laser therapy, shockwave, physical therapy) have a Q&A block. The auto-accidents page is the one that loses the most by not having one.

What you need: 6–8 FAQs per service page. For auto accidents: "Do I pay anything out of pocket after a Utah car accident?", "What is PIP and how does it work in Utah?", "Can I see a chiropractor before talking to my attorney?", "How long does whiplash recovery take?", "Do I need a referral?", "What if my injury was months ago?". Each gets a 2–3 sentence answer. FAQPage JSON-LD attached.

5. Sitemap is split four ways and your strongest pages are buried

You have four sub-sitemaps (sitemap.website.xml, sitemap.blog.xml, sitemap.ols.xml, sitemap.ola.xml). The main 13 service pages are in one, the 22 blog posts in another, two more for shop/online-learning artifacts that are probably empty. The lastmod date on every URL is identical (2026-05-17), which signals to crawlers that the site is bulk-generated. Crawl budget gets spent on shop URLs the customer never visits.

What you need: consolidate into a single sitemap.xml if your platform allows, or set realistic lastmod dates that match actual edit dates. Drop the empty sub-sitemaps. Submit the cleaned sitemap to Google Search Console and Bing Webmaster.

Bonus issue: Bing Places (powers ChatGPT local results)

ChatGPT uses Bing as its local-business backend when it does have web access. If your Bing Places listing is unclaimed, partially filled, or pointing to the wrong NAP, ChatGPT misses you even on retrieval queries. This takes about 30 minutes to fix and is free.

05 Tracking methodology, honest version (your #1 concern)

You wrote: "I already paid a company last year ... after 2 months they admitted they have no way to track my rankings on voice platforms." Read that twice. They sold you a measurement they could not deliver, then collected for two months before saying so. I will not do that. Here is the line between what is real and what is sales talk.

What I can actually measure for you, month after month

1. AI engine appearance rate (the test in this audit, re-run monthly)

Method: the same 6 queries × 4 engines = 24 runs you saw in section 1. Identical prompts, identical models, identical user-agent. Re-run on the 1st of each month. Save the raw JSON. Diff against last month.

What you see: "Last month: 1/24. This month: 4/24. Delta: +3. ChatGPT picked you up on the auto-accident query. Here is the exact answer."

Why it is real: the test is reproducible. The answers are saved. You can re-run it yourself if you ever want to verify. The previous vendor could not show you this because they were measuring something they did not actually query.

2. Google AI Overviews appearance (screenshot evidence)

Method: on the 1st of each month, search Google for the same 6 queries from a Provo IP. Capture screenshot of the AI Overview answer if one shows. Note whether Spine Craft Utah is cited as a source.

What you see: a folder of dated screenshots showing exactly what a Provo customer sees today vs three months ago.

Honest caveat: AI Overviews do not show for every query (Google decides). When they do show, your appearance as a citation is the win. When they do not, that query is on Google's list of "we will not generate AI for this" and you cannot force it.

3. GMB rating and review-count delta

Method: baseline today (5.0 stars / 31 reviews per the Provo profile). Re-pull from the Maps API on the 1st of each month. Track new reviews, sentiment shift, response rate.

What you see: "5.0 stars / 47 reviews this month, +16 since we started. 3 reviews mention auto accident, 2 mention Spanish-speaking patients."

Why it matters for AI: ChatGPT and Gemini pull GMB review volume and recency when they have access. More reviews and recent reviews = more likely to be cited.

4. Bing Places visibility (powers ChatGPT)

Method: claim and optimise the listing. Track the Bing-side impression count and click-through monthly (Bing Webmaster Tools provides this for free).

What you see: monthly Bing-impressions number. Most local businesses are at zero because the listing is unclaimed.

5. Plausible web analytics (privacy-friendly, no cookies)

Method: 1 line of script on every page. ~$9/mo direct to Plausible. Custom events on "tap to call", "book online click", "auto-accident page scroll-depth".

What you see: traffic source breakdown (Google, direct, AI engines that send referrals, social), top-pages-by-conversion, mobile vs desktop, before/after the work above.

Why this and not Google Analytics 4: GA4 is heavy, slow, and shows you patterns that are mostly noise for a single-location clinic. Plausible is small, fast, and shows the 4 numbers that matter.

What I will not pretend I can measure

Real-time voice assistant rankings

Siri, Alexa, Google Assistant. There is no public API. Anyone selling you a Siri-rank-tracker either does not have one or is sampling a small Siri query log they will not show you. The previous vendor admitted this after two months. I am telling you it now.

What I can do instead: the four AI-engine appearance rate above. When a customer asks Google Assistant "find me a chiropractor in Provo", Google Assistant queries the same underlying index Google Search and Gemini use. If we move you up in those, voice assistants follow. We just do not get a real-time scoreboard for the voice query specifically.

Per-customer attribution ("this patient came from Perplexity")

Patients usually do not remember which engine they used. UTM parameters work on link-clicks but break when the engine reads your answer aloud and the patient calls you directly. The honest version: ask the front desk to add one question on the intake form. "How did you hear about us?" with checkboxes for Google, ChatGPT, Friend, Insurance referral, Other. After two months you have signal.

Guaranteed first-page rankings or "X new patients in 60 days"

I do not guarantee these. Anyone who does is on the same path as your previous vendor. AI engines and Google update their algorithms weekly. What I guarantee is the work in section 6, the monthly scoreboard above, and that you will know what is shipping and what is not every single month.

07 Examples and LLM prompts you can use today

Everything below is copy-pasteable. Three prompt blocks. Open Claude or ChatGPT, paste, answer the questions it asks, get a first draft you can adapt.

Prompt 1 — Draft the FAQ block for your auto-accidents page

Examples of what good looks like (auto-accident chiropractic FAQ)

  • "Do I pay anything out of pocket after a Utah car accident?" Most patients pay nothing. Utah law requires every vehicle owner to carry $3,000 in Personal Injury Protection (PIP). We bill PIP directly. If more care is needed past the PIP cap, we work with your attorney under a Doctor's Lien arrangement, meaning you only pay if and when a settlement is reached.
  • "How soon after my accident should I come in?" Within 72 hours is ideal. Soft-tissue inflammation peaks at 48–72 hours, so an early baseline lets us prevent the injury from settling into a chronic pattern. If the accident was weeks or months ago and pain is lingering, you are not too late, the Croft Guidelines protocol still applies.
  • "Do I need a referral from my doctor or attorney?" No. Walk-ins are welcome. If you already have an attorney, we coordinate billing directly with their office.
Copy this prompt
You: help me write 6 FAQ entries for my chiropractic clinic's auto-accident page in Provo, UT.

Context:
- Clinic: Spine Craft Utah, 1807 N 1120 W, Provo
- Doctor: Dr. Rob Wheeler, DC (30 yrs experience, Expert Medical Witness)
- Niche: Utah motor vehicle accident injury recovery
- Billing: PIP first ($3,000 Utah mandatory), then Doctor's Lien w/ attorney
- Protocol: Croft Guidelines (Grade I-V severity, acute/subacute/chronic stages)
- Offer: $47 new-patient special, walk-ins welcome

Ask me 4 q's first:
1. Most common patient confusion you hear at the front desk?
2. Question attorneys most often ask before referring?
3. 1-2 specific Utah-only facts to mention (laws, insurance, courts)?
4. Tone: matter-of-fact medical or warm patient-first?

Then draft 6 FAQs. Each: 1 question + 2-3 sentence answer.
Active voice. No em-dashes. No marketing language ("amazing", "world-class").
Output as plain text, one Q+A per paragraph.

Prompt 2 — Draft Dr. Wheeler's team-page bio

Examples of what good looks like (independent-clinic doctor bio)

  • Specific year + place opener. "Dr. Rob Wheeler has practiced chiropractic care since 1998. He opened Spine Craft Utah in Provo after running clinics in Alaska and Florida." Beats "Dr. Wheeler is a passionate chiropractor with decades of experience."

In practice, this means he reads the imaging the same way the insurance company's reviewer reads it." Beats "highly trained in advanced techniques."
  • What the patient gets, not what the doctor does. "On a first visit you get the imaging review, the Croft severity grade, and a written treatment frequency. You leave knowing what care looks like for the next 4 weeks." Beats "comprehensive initial consultation."
